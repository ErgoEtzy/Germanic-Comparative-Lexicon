# *fulką

[Alphabetical index](../_Index.md) · [View on Wiktionary](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/fulk%C4%85)

## Proto-Germanic

**Reconstruction:** *fulką

### Etymology

From *fulkaz (“crowd, army”), of uncertain origin. May ultimately be from Proto-Indo-European *pl̥h₁-gós, from *pleh₁- (“to fill”). Compare Latin plebs (“common people”).
Descendants of *pъlkъ (“troop”), such as Old Church Slavonic плъкъ (plŭkŭ, “army division”), show f > p substitution, which simply could be a result of the word being borrowed before that sound became a standard phoneme in the languages thanks to loanwords from Greek and Latin (later solidified from more loans featuring f from other languages as well). Some also speculate the possibility of the word being borrowed before Grimm's law even started, but the presence of voiceless -k- in place of -g- would disprove this, (otherwise, *pъlgъ would be expected). Romanian
pâlc (“herd, flock”) and Lithuanian pulkas (“crowd”) are borrowed from Slavic.
Orel's connection with Welsh ôl (“track, trace”) is unlikely, considering the latter's likely origin from Proto-Indo-European *h₂el- (“alien, other”).

### Proto-Indo-European ancestry

**Directly mentioned PIE form(s):** *h₂el-, *pl̥h₁-gós
