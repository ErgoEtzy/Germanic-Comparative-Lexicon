## Proto-Germanic

**Reconstruction:** *smikraz

### Etymology

Uncertain. Possibly from Proto-Indo-European *smeyg- or *smeyǵ- (“to jab; to languish, be small or pitiful”) (maybe an extension of *smey- (“to smear, stroke, wipe, rub”); compare *smītaną), + *-raz; compare Lithuanian smeigti (“to lunge, thrust, jab”), šmižti (“to remain small”), susmi̇̀žęs (“crippled, small”) and Latvian smidzis (“mite, water flea”). The usual assumption is of an earlier meaning “small, delicate” for the Proto-Germanic adjective, which is a widely paralleled semantic development.
Often compared (cf. Pokorny) also to Ancient Greek σμῑκρός (smīkrós), μῑκρός (mīkrós, “small, short”) and Latin mīca (“crumb, morsel, bit”), as if for Proto-Indo-European *(s)mey(h₁)- (“small”), yet such a relationship is formally impossible—and the Greek words may even be Pre-Greek for this reason in conjunction with additional variant forms that are hard to explain. Nonetheless, V. Oryol considers these words related “[d]espite the irregular *-k-” in Germanic. Also traditionally connected to Proto-Germanic *smēhaz (“small, little”) and/or *smalaz (id.), but these are likewise not derivable from a common root. They appear to constitute a sound-symbolic set, which could explain the similar terms in Latin and Greek, as well as Proto-Slavic *smagnǫti (“to wither”), etc., despite the incompatible phonetics beyond sm-.
