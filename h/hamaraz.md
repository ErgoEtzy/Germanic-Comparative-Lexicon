## Proto-Germanic

**Reconstruction:** *hamaraz

### Etymology

Traditionally treated as derived from Proto-Indo-European *h₂éḱmō (“stone”) (compare Sanskrit अश्मन् (aśman, “stone”)), from *h₂eḱ- (“sharp”), via a formation like *h₂eḱmoros (compare Sanskrit अश्मर (aśmará, “stony”)). But the phonology is problematic. A metathesis of *h₂ and ḱ like that assumed to underlie Proto-Balto-Slavic *kā́ˀmō may account for the Germanic short vowel, i.e. *ḱh₂moros. Hyllested instead suggests borrowing from Proto-Finnic *hamara (“butt of an axe, back of a knife”). However, it is not clear that the proposed chronology works out: At the implied time of borrowing (last centuries BC or first centuries AD), Finnic *h may well still have been *š, which would probably be reflected as *s in Germanic. Some state this being either a substrate word from a lost language, derived from an onomatopoeiac root, or both, but this is no longer suggested today, since the Indo-Europeans knew how to make hammers with stone by the time Germanic split off, so it made more sense that they would use some variant of Proto-Indo-European *h₂éḱmō to describe the tool instead. The link to the roots *kem (“to cover, to clothe”) (where the meaning of hammer would be "the thing covered with a handle) or *h₂em (“to press, force”) (where the meaning would be "the thing that strikes") is most likely a phonological coincidence, and thus is not taken seriously by most.
