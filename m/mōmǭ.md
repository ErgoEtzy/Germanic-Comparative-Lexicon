# *mōmǭ

[Alphabetical index](../_Index.md) · [View on Wiktionary](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/m%C5%8Dm%C7%AD)

## Proto-Germanic

**Reconstruction:** *mōmǭ

### Etymology

Perhaps from Proto-Indo-European *méh₂-(m)meh₂-, which appears to be a reduplication of a base *meh₂- (“to nurse; mama, mother”) found also in *méh₂tēr. Cognate with Latin mamma (“mother, mama, nurse; mother's breast”), Ancient Greek μάμμη (mámmē, “mother, mama; mother's breast”), Proto-Balto-Slavic *mā́ˀmāˀ (Lithuanian mãma, momà (“mother”), Latvian mãma (“mother”), Proto-Slavic *màma (“mommy”)), and Proto-Celtic *mammā (Old Irish muimme (“foster mother”), Irish mam (“mother”), Scottish Gaelic mamaidh (“mommy”), Breton mamm (“mother; womb”)). However, despite the apparent formal relation, independent nursery word (Lallwort) origins for many or all of these words are likely, as "baby talk" similar to momma/mama is found worldwide and historically is observed to frequently reshape words contrary to the regular sound laws. The frequent occurrence of gemination in these words is a well-documented phenomenon of Lallwörter and hypocorisms, akin to that seen in the synonym *ammǭ as well as *attô (“father, daddy, papa”), Icelandic babbi and Old Frisian bobba (“brother”); further compare unrelated Sanskrit अक्का (akkā, “mother”), Bengali আম্মা (amma), Icelandic mamma, and Afrikaans mamma. Nonetheless, the Germanic root found in this word and in *mōijǭ interestingly appears to show the regular change of PIE *eh₂ to PGmc. *ō, and the accent in Baltic may prove the original laryngeal quality.
